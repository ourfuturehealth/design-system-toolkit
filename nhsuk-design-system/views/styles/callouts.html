Warning

Usage

The warning callout can be used to display information that is:

  • time critical
  • may have a significant health impact
  • addresses a common and/or significant misconception or patient mistake

User need

As a user I need to identify and understand the important content on the page (even if I don't read all the content) so that I get the best outcome for my situation and don't endanger my health.

Design iterations

Version 1

Don't ignore an infected blister. Without treatment it could lead to a skin or blood infection.

Version 2

Hypothesis

We believe that by making the yellow brighter and increasing the font weight, will make it look more like a warning and people will stop and read it.

Don't ignore an infected blister. Without treatment it could lead to a skin or blood infection.

Lab user research - 13/6/2018
Findings
  • 3/5 five users initially missed the callout, with one indicating their attention was drawn to the heading below.
  • Most users correctly understood its importance vs other call outs and the meaning of the yellow colour as a warning.

Version 3

Hypothesis

We believe that users scan headings. Adding a heading will make users stop and take notice of the callout. Also, changing the background colour to yellow will make it stand out from the surrounding white boxes.

Key point

111 Care Card

Usage

The 111 care card can be used to direct users to the NHS 111 helpline if they need non-emergency help or advice and don't have access to a health professional or GP.

User need

As a user I NEED to understand my options if I don't know what to do and I can't speak to my GP, SO THAT I can get the best outcome for my situation.

Example

Call 111

If you can't speak to your GP and don't know what to do next.

Primary Care Card

Usage

The primary care card can be used to instruct users to visit a health professional or GP if appropriate to their situation.

User need

As a user I NEED to understand what to do if my symptoms indicate I need to see a GP, SO THAT I can get the best outcome for my situation.

Example

Speak to a GP if:

  • you're not sure it's chickenpox
  • the skin around the blisters is red, hot or painful (signs of infection)
  • your child is dehydrated
  • you're concerned about your child or they get worse

Tell the receptionist you think it's chickenpox before going in. They may recommend a special appointment time if other patients are at risk.

Urgent Care Card

Usage

The urgent care card can be used to instruct users to visit a GP or hospital urgently if appropriate to their situation.

User need

As a user I NEED to understand what to do if my symptoms indicate I need urgent GP care SO THAT I can get the best outcome for my situation.

Example

Ask for an urgent GP appointment if:

  • you're an adult and have chickenpox
  • you're pregnant and haven't had chickenpox before and you've been near someone with it
  • you have a weakened immune system and you've been near someone with chickenpox
  • you think your newborn baby has chickenpox

In these situations, your GP can prescribe medicine to prevent complications. You need to take it within 24 hours of the spots coming out.

Emergency Care Card

Usage

The emergency care card can be used to instruct users to immediately seek emergency help if appropriate to their situation.

User need

As a user I NEED to understand what to do if my symptoms indicate I need to go straight to hospital SO THAT I can get the best outcome for my situation.

Example

Call 999 if you have sudden chest pain that:

  • spreads to your arms, back, neck or jaw
  • makes your chest feel tight or heavy
  • also started with shortness of breath, sweating and feeling or being sick

You could be having a heart attack. Call 999 immediately as you need immediate treatment in hospital.
