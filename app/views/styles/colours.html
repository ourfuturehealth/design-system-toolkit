Colour contrast

You must make sure that the contrast ratio of text and elements meets level AA of the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG 2.0)

You can check colour contrasts online using:

  • Web Aim's Colour Contrast Checker
  • Color Oracle (free colour blindness simulator)

As well as using these tools, test colour contrast with people of all abilities.

Main palette

Brand colours

  • NHS Blue is the dominant colour in the NHS colour palette. See header, links, care cards and key point
  • White is used to make important information stand out and for alternating backgrounds

UI colours

  • Green is used in action buttons and links, see action link
  • Purple is used for visited links
  • Red is used for urgency and negative or destructive actions, see care cards
  • Yellow is used in warnings, see callouts
  • Warm yellow is used for focus states, see focus colour

Greyscale

  • Black is used for headings and the main body copy, see text colours
  • Grey 1 is used for secondary text, see text colours
  • Grey 2, 3 and 4 are used for borders
  • Grey 5 (page tint) is used for page backgrounds

We use a page tint because...

  • It gives us greater editorial flexibility - many pages have multiple callout boxes, and this allows us to pop important info out in white
  • It reduces page glare for people in low-light conditions (e.g. Lying in bed looking at the Insomnia page at 3am)
  • The British Dyslexia Association's 2018 guide to accessible content recommends dark text on a light (but not white) background
